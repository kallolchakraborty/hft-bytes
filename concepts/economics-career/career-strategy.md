## Key Learning Points

- **Firm type comparison**: the four categories of HFT employer: (a) **Prop trading shops** (Jane Street, Citadel Securities, Jump, DRW, IMC, Hudson River, Tower) — pure PnL focus, best technology, most latency-sensitive, highest comp at senior levels, but highest pressure. (b) **Banks** (Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan) — slower technology, more bureaucracy, but better work-life balance, strong brand, benefits. (c) **Market makers** (Susquehanna, Optiver, Flow Traders) — hybrid of prop shop culture with more structure, strong risk management. (d) **Vendors** (TradingTech, Exegy, Nasdaq Technology) — sell software/hardware, less comp, lower pressure, more stable. (e) **Exchanges** (CME, ICE, Nasdaq) — slowest technology, government-regulated, best stability, moderate comp. For a Senior+ dev: prop shops offer the highest total comp ($500K-$2M+) and most interesting technical challenges, but require the most commitment
- **Compensation structure**: three components: (a) base salary ($200K-$400K for senior, depends on location and firm); (b) annual bonus (50-200% of base, tied to firm PnL and individual performance); (c) carried interest / profit share (only at prop shops and some market makers, can be 10-30% of the firm's PnL allocated to a trading desk or team). Structure varies: banks pay 60-70% base (higher base, lower bonus ratio); prop shops pay 20-30% base (lower base, higher bonus/carry). Key questions to ask: "What was the bonus range for my level over the last 3 years?" and "Do senior engineers get carry?" and "Is carry paid annually or at exit?"
- **Negotiation tactics specific to HFT**: (a) get multiple offers — prop shops hire in clusters, run interviews in parallel; (b) use competing prop shop offers against each other (they expect this); (c) negotiate signing bonus (1-2 years of unvested comp from your current firm is a legitimate reason for a larger signing bonus); (d) negotiate non-compete duration (shorter is better, 6 months vs 12 months can make a huge difference); (e) ask about "guarantee" — first-year comp guarantee is common (the firm guarantees X amount total comp in year 1, even if PnL is low). Don't negotiate base salary alone — focus on total comp (base + bonus + signing + carry). Timing: negotiate in writing, after the verbal offer
- **Non-compete strategy**: HFT non-competes are serious (typically 6-12 months, enforced). Some states (California, Oklahoma, North Dakota) ban non-competes; most others enforce them. Non-compete terms: cannot work for a "competitive firm" (defined broadly — any trading firm in the same asset class) for the duration. Strategies: (a) negotiate gardening leave (firm pays you during non-compete) — standard in London, rare in US; (b) negotiate a "non-compete reduction" clause — if you leave voluntarily and repay signing bonus, non-compete is reduced to 3 months; (c) switch asset classes (equities MM → crypto MM is not the same market, so non-compete may not apply); (d) relocate to a state without non-compete enforcement (California, Colorado). If you have a non-compete: get a lawyer who specializes in HFT employment law (not general employment lawyer). Expect the previous firm to enforce — hiring firms often escrow a portion of your comp for non-compete risk
- **How to evaluate a firm's technology**: the technology stack is a proxy for the engineering culture. Ask: (a) "What version of Linux is your trading server running?" (expect RHEL 9 or Rocky 9, not Ubuntu with old kernel); (b) "Do you use DPDK or Solarflare Onload?" (kernel bypass is non-negotiable); (c) "What's your build system?" (Bazel or CMake, not Makefiles); (d) "How do you test changes?" (deterministic replay, CI with perf regression gates, not manual testing); (e) "How many lines of code in the core trading system?" (reasonable = 100K-1M, bloated = > 5M); (f) "Do you have a dedicated infrastructure team?" (yes = you focus on trading logic, no = you fix network cables too). Visit the office: observe colo site vs HQ, ask about on-call rotation (nightmare if non-existent). Ask the team: "What's the most recent production incident and how did you fix it?" — the answer reveals the engineering maturity
- **Career progression**: typical path at a prop shop: (a) **Junior** (0-3 years) — implement features, fix bugs, learn the codebase, $200-350K total comp. (b) **Mid** (3-6 years) — own a subsystem (feed handler, OMS, SOR), lead small projects, $350-600K. (c) **Senior** (6-10 years) — architect new systems, mentor juniors, drive performance improvements, $500-1M. (d) **Staff+** (10+ years) — cross-team architecture, design firm-wide systems, direct production strategy, comp tied to trading desk PnL, $1M-5M+. (e) **Partner** (rare) — equity in the firm, board-level decisions, comp = % of firm PnL. Progression depends on: technical impact, trading PnL contribution, ability to reduce risk, mentorship/leadership. Benchmark: after 5 years at a top firm, you should either be a Staff+ or have an offer to be Staff+ elsewhere
- **When to leave**: signs it's time to move: (a) your learning has plateaued (you master the system and the problems are repetitive); (b) your comp is below market for your output (check with recruiters — don't ask your manager); (c) the technology is stagnant (no investment in new hardware/software); (d) the team is shrinking (layoffs or attrition without replacement); (e) you're on the wrong desk (options MM on the floor vs equities at an exchange — switch desks before switching firms). When leaving: give standard notice (2 weeks in US, 1-3 months in UK/EU), do not badmouth, do not take source code (illegal and likely to result in legal action against you), maintain relationships (HFT is a small world — everyone knows everyone)
- **Tax considerations for HFT comp**: bonus and carry are taxed as ordinary income (top marginal rate: 37% US federal + state + city). In high-tax states (NY, CA, NJ), marginal rate can exceed 50%. Strategies: (a) negotiate some comp as long-term capital gains (carry/fund interest, taxed at 20%); (b) if your firm allows it, defer bonus into future years (deferral can reduce tax bracket); (c) move to a tax-friendly state (Florida, Texas, Nevada, Washington) — no state income tax. Many prop shops have offices in Austin, Miami, Seattle for this reason. Non-compete payments during gardening leave are taxable income

## Staff+ Perspective

> **Staff+ Perspective**: The three biggest career lessons I've learned in HFT: First, **comp at prop shops is tied to firm PnL**, not your personal output. I had a year where my project saved the firm $2M (detecting a matching engine bug before it caused losses), but the firm had a bad year overall (strategy losses) and my bonus was 50% of the previous year. Conversely, I know a developer who joined a firm in a bad year, did average work, and got a $1M bonus because the firm had a record year. PnL luck dominates individual performance in any given year — evaluate your comp over 3+ year rolling average, not year-to-year. Second, **technology stack quality is the best predictor of engineering culture**. I interviewed at a firm that bragged about their "low-latency C++" — then mentioned they compile with `-O0` for debugging. I passed on them. The best firms invest heavily in tooling, testing, and build infrastructure. Third, **non-competes are not theoretical**. I've seen colleagues: (a) successfully non-competed — spent 12 months at home on full pay, couldn't trade; (b) sued for violating — previous firm won a $500K judgment; (c) hired with escrow — new firm set aside $200K in case of legal action. Get a lawyer. My advice for mid-career devs: optimize for (a) learning rate — work at the firm with the best technology and hardest problems; (b) network — build relationships with senior traders and quants (they are the ones who will hire you later); (c) PnL contribution — get your code connected to a PnL line; (d) optionality — keep your resume updated, talk to recruiters every 6 months even if you're happy. The best time to find a new role is when you don't need one.
